Epoch I
Creation
Genesis 1–2 · The Beginning of All Things
Day 1–6 Creation of the Heavens and Earth ▸

God creates the heavens and the earth ex nihilo by the Word of His power. Over six days He forms light, sky, land, vegetation, celestial bodies, sea creatures, land animals, and finally man—male and female—in His own image (Gen 1:1–31). Each creative act proceeds by divine fiat: "And God said." The creation is declared "very good."

▸ Covenant of Works

God enters a covenant with Adam as the federal head of the human race. The terms: perfect, personal, and perpetual obedience, symbolically summarized in the prohibition of the tree of knowledge. The promise: confirmed, eschatological life—glorified, unlosable communion with God. The sanction: death in its fullest sense—spiritual, physical, and eternal. Adam stands as covenant representative of all his natural posterity. Without this covenant, the entire structure of federal theology—and the necessity of Christ's active obedience—becomes unintelligible.

▸ Kingdom of God

Creation is the establishment of God's kingdom. He reigns as sovereign Creator. Adam is installed as vice-regent—the image-bearing son—tasked with the cultural mandate: be fruitful, multiply, fill the earth, exercise dominion (Gen 1:28). The garden is the original sanctuary, the dwelling-place of God with man. The Edenic kingdom is the archetypal pattern for all subsequent development of the Kingdom of God.

▸ Types & Shadows

Adam as type of Christ: Explicitly identified as "a type of the one who was to come" (Rom 5:14). As the first Adam was federal head of the old creation in the covenant of works, so Christ—the last Adam—is the federal head of the new creation in the covenant of grace.

Eden as temple-archetype: The garden possesses features anticipating the tabernacle and temple: eastward orientation, guarding cherubim, tree of life, God's special presence, and Adam's priestly vocation to "work and keep" (the same Hebrew terms used for Levitical service). This culminates in the New Jerusalem (Rev 21–22).

Sabbath as eschatological type: The seventh-day rest is a type of consummation rest awaiting the people of God (Heb 4:1–11). Work followed by rest, probation followed by confirmation, labor followed by glory.

Epoch II
The Fall
Genesis 3 · The Catastrophe of Sin
Genesis 3 The Temptation and Transgression ▸

The serpent—an instrument of Satan—tempts Eve through distortion of God's Word. She takes the fruit and eats; Adam, present with her, receives it from her hand and eats in deliberate, representative transgression. Shame, guilt, alienation, and spiritual death immediately ensue.

▸ Breach of the Covenant of Works

Adam, as federal head, fails the probationary test. His sin is imputed to all posterity (Rom 5:12–21). Death in all dimensions falls on the entire race. Total depravity pervades every faculty. Man is now unable to keep the covenant of works yet remains bound by its demands.

Gen 3:15 Proto-Evangelium — The First Gospel Promise ▸

"I will put enmity between you and the woman, and between your offspring and her offspring; he shall bruise your head, and you shall bruise his heel."

This is the proto-evangelium—the first gospel proclamation. It promises a male descendant of the woman who will decisively crush the serpent, though suffering in the process. This promise is developed through the Abrahamic seed, the Davidic heir, and ultimately fulfilled in Christ's atoning death and victorious resurrection.

▸ Inauguration of the Covenant of Grace

In the midst of judgment, God announces grace. This marks the historical inauguration of the Covenant of Grace—the gracious arrangement whereby God promises salvation through a Mediator to all who believe. This covenant is, in its substance, one covenant administered in various forms throughout Old and New Testaments. The antithesis between the seed of the woman and the seed of the serpent structures all subsequent redemptive history.

▸ Types & Shadows

Garments of skin (Gen 3:21): God provides covering through the death of an animal—the first shedding of blood and a type of substitutionary atonement. The innocent dying to cover the guilty anticipates the sacrificial system and Christ's righteousness clothing His people (Isa 61:10; Rev 7:14).

Expulsion as exile-type: Banishment from Eden is the first exile. Sin leads to removal from God's presence—a pattern recurring at Babel, in the wilderness, and supremely in the Babylonian exile. Christ's crucifixion outside the camp is the ultimate exile; His resurrection the ultimate return.

Epoch III
Primeval History
Genesis 4–11 · From the First Promise to the Call of Abraham
Gen 4 Cain & Abel — The Two Seeds ▸

The antithesis between the two seeds immediately unfolds. Abel offers an acceptable sacrifice by faith (Heb 11:4); Cain brings an unacceptable offering. Cain murders Abel—the first instance of the serpent's seed persecuting the woman's seed. Yet God preserves the godly line through Seth ("appointed"), through whom the seed-promise continues.

▸ Abel as Type of Christ

The righteous sufferer, killed by his brother, whose blood cries from the ground (Gen 4:10; Heb 12:24). Christ's blood speaks a "better word" than the blood of Abel.

Gen 6–9 The Flood — Judgment & Preservation ▸

Universal wickedness provokes divine judgment. God destroys the world by a global deluge but preserves Noah and his family—eight souls—through the ark.

▸ Noahic Covenant (Gen 8:20–9:17)

God establishes a covenant with Noah and all creation, promising never again to destroy the earth by flood. The rainbow is the sign. This universal, preservation covenant sustains the stage of history upon which redemption will unfold. Noah receives a renewed cultural mandate (Gen 9:1–7), with the addition of capital punishment as a safeguard of the image of God.

▸ Types & Shadows

Noah as type of Christ: The righteous man through whom a remnant is saved from universal judgment. The ark is a type of Christ and the Church—divinely provided deliverance from God's wrath. Peter connects the flood-waters to baptism (1 Pet 3:20–21).

Flood as de-creation/re-creation: Waters cover the earth as in the primordial deep, then recede to reveal dry land. Judgment-through-water leading to new creation anticipates the Red Sea, baptism, and the final cosmic renewal.

Gen 10–11 Table of Nations & Tower of Babel ▸

The Table of Nations (Gen 10) establishes the seventy nations from Noah's sons—Shem, Ham, Japheth—the geopolitical backdrop for all Scripture. At Babel (Gen 11), humanity rebels by consolidating power in defiance of the mandate to fill the earth. God confuses their language and scatters them—a judgment that also preserves the nations through which His redemptive purposes will work.

▸ Babel as Anti-type of Pentecost

At Babel, language is confused as judgment. At Pentecost (Acts 2), the Spirit enables communication across all languages—the reversal of Babel's curse and the beginning of the eschatological ingathering of the nations.

Epoch IV
The Patriarchal Era
Genesis 12–50 · c. 2091–1876 BC · Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Joseph
c. 2091 BC The Call of Abraham ▸

God calls Abram out of Ur of the Chaldees—out of paganism—and promises him a land, a seed, and a blessing extending to all nations (Gen 12:1–3). This is the decisive narrowing: God's redemptive purposes now focus on one family. Location: Ur → Haran → Canaan (Shechem, Bethel, Hebron, Beersheba).

▸ The Abrahamic Covenant

An administration of the Covenant of Grace with three central promises: (1) Seed—a great nation, ultimately the singular Seed, Christ (Gal 3:16); (2) Land—Canaan as typological inheritance pointing to the new creation (Heb 11:10, 16); (3) Blessing—through Abraham's seed all families blessed, the promise of justification by faith for the Gentiles (Gal 3:8). Circumcision is the covenant sign—pointing to circumcision of the heart (Deut 30:6; Col 2:11–12).

c. 2081 BC Covenant Ceremony — God's Self-Maledictory Oath ▸

In Gen 15, God passes between the severed animals in a self-maledictory oath, taking the covenant curse upon Himself—a stunning foreshadowing of the cross. God alone walks between the pieces; the covenant's fulfillment rests entirely on divine faithfulness. In Gen 17, circumcision is formally instituted as the covenant sign.

c. 2054 BC The Binding of Isaac (Aqedah) ▸

God commands Abraham to sacrifice his only son Isaac on Mount Moriah—the future site of the Jerusalem temple and Golgotha. Abraham obeys in faith; God provides a ram as substitute.

▸ Types & Shadows

Abraham as type of the Father: Willingness to offer the beloved son (John 3:16). Isaac as type of Christ: The beloved son, child of promise, carried the wood of his own sacrifice up the mountain, bound upon the altar, received back "in a figure" from the dead (Heb 11:17–19). The ram is a type of substitutionary atonement.

Gen 14 Melchizedek — The Priest-King of Salem ▸

King of Salem and priest of God Most High—a king-priest who blesses Abraham and receives tithes. A type of Christ's eternal, royal priesthood that supersedes the Aaronic order (Ps 110:4; Heb 5–7). The union of kingly and priestly offices in one person anticipates Christ's mediatorial work.

Gen 25–36 Jacob — Election & the Twelve Tribes ▸

Jacob, chosen over Esau before birth (Rom 9:10–13), receives the covenant blessing. His twelve sons become the twelve tribes of Israel. The seed promise narrows to Judah: "The scepter shall not depart from Judah" (Gen 49:10)—the earliest explicit prediction of a royal Messiah from a specific tribe.

c. 1898 BC Joseph — Suffering & Exaltation ▸

Beloved of the father, hated and rejected by his brothers, sold for silver, consigned to suffering, exalted to the right hand of power, and savior of his people and the nations. Joseph's suffering and exaltation providentially bring the family to Egypt, setting the stage for the Exodus.

▸ Joseph as Type of Christ

One of the most extended Christological types in the Old Testament. Rejected → suffering → exaltation → salvation of the very ones who rejected him. "You meant evil against me, but God meant it for good" (Gen 50:20)—the clearest Old Testament statement of God's sovereign providence over human sin for redemptive purposes.

Epoch V
Exodus & Sinai
Exodus–Deuteronomy · c. 1446–1406 BC
c. 1446 BC The Exodus — Deliverance from Egypt ▸

After 430 years in Egypt—the last generations in bondage—God raises up Moses. The burning bush theophany reveals the divine name YHWH (Exod 3:14). Ten plagues demonstrate YHWH's sovereignty over Egypt's gods. The Passover (Exod 12): blood of the lamb on doorposts, death of Egypt's firstborn, redemption of Israel. Crossing of the Red Sea—divine deliverance through water and judgment.

▸ Types & Shadows

Moses as type of Christ: The great mediator, deliverer, prophet, lawgiver. Christ is the greater Moses (Deut 18:15; Heb 3:1–6).

Passover lamb: "Christ our Passover lamb has been sacrificed" (1 Cor 5:7). Unblemished lamb, blood applied, angel of death passing over—a direct type of Christ's atoning death.

Red Sea crossing: Salvation through water and judgment, connected to baptism (1 Cor 10:1–2). A new-creation event: Israel passes through waters of chaos into new life.

▸ Geopolitical Context

Egypt is the dominant world empire. Israel's deliverance is the Old Testament's paradigmatic act of redemption—God defeating the world power to free His people. Egypt remains a symbol of bondage throughout Scripture (Rev 11:8).

c. 1446 BC Sinai — The Mosaic Covenant & the Law ▸

God descends on Sinai in fire, smoke, and thunder. The Ten Commandments—the moral law summarized—are spoken by God and written on stone. The civil and ceremonial law are given. Israel is constituted as "a kingdom of priests and a holy nation" (Exod 19:6).

▸ The Mosaic Covenant

A new administration of the Covenant of Grace layered on the Abrahamic. It has the form of a suzerainty treaty: the great King (YHWH) binds His vassal people (Israel) with stipulations (the law), blessings for obedience, curses for disobedience (Lev 26; Deut 28). The Mosaic covenant does not replace the Abrahamic promise (Gal 3:17) but serves a pedagogical and typological function: the law reveals sin, drives to Christ (Gal 3:24), and provides the ceremonial framework that pictures gospel realities.

c. 1445 BC The Tabernacle — God Dwells Among His People ▸

"Let them make me a sanctuary, that I may dwell in their midst" (Exod 25:8). The tabernacle is a portable temple—a microcosm of creation and a foretaste of the new heavens and earth.

▸ Tabernacle Typology

The layout, furniture (ark, table, lampstand, altar), the veil, the Holy of Holies—all point to Christ, in whom God "tabernacles" among us (John 1:14). Day of Atonement (Lev 16): The high priest enters the Holy of Holies once a year with blood—type of Christ's once-for-all entrance into the heavenly sanctuary (Heb 9:11–12). The scapegoat bearing sins away typifies complete removal of guilt.

Manna: Bread from heaven (John 6:31–35). Water from the rock: The rock was Christ (1 Cor 10:4). Bronze serpent: "As Moses lifted up the serpent, so must the Son of Man be lifted up" (John 3:14).

1446–1406 BC Wilderness Wanderings — 40 Years of Testing ▸

Israel's unbelief at Kadesh-Barnea (Num 14) results in 40 years of wilderness wandering. The first generation, except Caleb and Joshua, dies in the wilderness. Rebellion of Korah. Moses strikes the rock in anger and is barred from the land. Deuteronomic covenant renewal on the plains of Moab. Death of Moses. The wilderness is a period of testing, chastening, and divine provision—Israel's 40 years recapitulated in Christ's 40-day temptation, where He succeeds where Israel failed.

Epoch VI
Conquest & Judges
Joshua–Ruth · c. 1406–1050 BC
c. 1406 BC Joshua — Conquest of Canaan ▸

Joshua leads Israel across the Jordan into Canaan. Fall of Jericho. Holy war: God gives the land as promised. Allotment of tribal territories. Joshua's covenant renewal at Shechem (Josh 24). The land promise is partially fulfilled (Josh 21:43–45), though the conquest is incomplete.

▸ Joshua as Type of Christ

Joshua (Yehoshua = "YHWH saves"—the same name as Jesus) leads God's people into the promised rest that Moses could not provide (Heb 4:8). The conquering warrior who secures the inheritance. The land-rest typology anticipates a greater, eschatological rest (Heb 4).

c. 1380–1050 BC The Judges — Cycles of Sin & Deliverance ▸

Recurring cycle: sin → servitude → supplication → salvation. God raises judges as temporary, Spirit-empowered deliverers: Othniel, Ehud, Deborah, Gideon, Jephthah, Samson, and others. Moral and spiritual decline: "Everyone did what was right in his own eyes" (Judg 21:25). The period demonstrates that even with the land, the law, and the cultus, Israel cannot maintain fidelity without a righteous king—and ultimately without the new heart only the New Covenant provides.

▸ Kingdom of God — The Need for a King

"There was no king in Israel" (Judg 17:6; 21:25)—a theological statement: the kingdom of God requires a king. This creates the longing for the Davidic monarchy and ultimately the Messianic King. Geopolitical pressures from Canaanites, Moab, Ammon, Midian, and especially Philistia underscore the need.

c. 1100 BC Ruth & Boaz — The Kinsman-Redeemer ▸

Within the darkness of the judges, a counter-narrative of covenant faithfulness. Boaz redeems Ruth and her inheritance—a type of Christ as kinsman-redeemer who acquires His bride and secures her inheritance. Ruth, a Moabitess, is a Gentile grafted into the covenant community, foreshadowing the inclusion of the nations. She enters the Davidic and Messianic genealogy (Matt 1:5). Bethlehem. Hannah's song (1 Sam 2) anticipates the anointed king and foreshadows the Magnificat (Luke 1:46–55).

Epoch VII
The United Monarchy
1 Samuel–1 Kings 11 · c. 1050–931 BC
c. 1050–1010 BC Saul — A King After the People's Heart ▸

Israel demands a king "like all the nations" (1 Sam 8:5). Saul: physically imposing, initially Spirit-empowered, but faithless. Unlawful sacrifices (1 Sam 13), disobedience on Amalek (1 Sam 15), consults the medium at Endor (1 Sam 28). The Spirit departs. Dies defeated on Gilboa. A cautionary type: human kingship apart from divine faithfulness.

▸ Prophets: Samuel

Last judge and first classical prophet. Bridges the judges and the monarchy. Anoints both Saul and David. Embodies the prophetic office that will check and guide the monarchy throughout its history.

c. 1010–970 BC David — A Man After God's Own Heart ▸

Anointed by Samuel as a youth. Defeats Goliath. Flees from Saul. King first over Judah at Hebron, then all Israel. Conquers Jerusalem—political and religious capital. Brings the ark to Jerusalem. David's sins (Bathsheba, the census) bring severe consequences, yet God's covenant remains firm.

▸ The Davidic Covenant (2 Sam 7)

The climactic Old Testament covenant. God promises: (1) a son who will build the temple; (2) an eternal throne and kingdom; (3) a father-son relationship with David's heir. Unconditional and irrevocable (Ps 89:34–37). The foundation of all subsequent messianic expectation. Fulfilled in Christ, the Son of David, whose throne is eternal (Luke 1:32–33; Acts 2:29–36).

▸ David as Type of Christ

The shepherd-king, anointed and rejected before coming to power, defeats the enemy champion, establishes the kingdom, receives the eternal covenant. Christ is "the Root and Offspring of David" (Rev 22:16). David's psalms shape Israel's worship and provide the vocabulary for messianic expectation. Prophets: Nathan (Davidic covenant oracle, 2 Sam 7; confrontation, 2 Sam 12) and Gad (David's seer).

c. 970–931 BC Solomon — Temple, Wisdom, & Apostasy ▸

Receives wisdom from God. Builds the temple on Mount Moriah (2 Chr 3:1)—the permanent dwelling of God. Temple dedication (1 Kgs 8): God's glory fills the house. Solomon's wealth, wisdom, and fame represent the kingdom's zenith. Yet foreign wives lead to idolatry (1 Kgs 11). God announces the kingdom's division.

▸ Temple & Solomon as Types

The Temple: God's permanent dwelling, built on Moriah (site of the Aqedah). Type of Christ's body (John 2:19–21), the Church (Eph 2:19–22), and the heavenly reality (Heb 8:1–5). Architecture recapitulates Eden: cherubim, palm trees, open flowers (1 Kgs 6:29).

Solomon as type: Son of David who builds the temple, reigns in peace, exercises unsurpassed wisdom, sees nations stream to him. Yet his failures show he is not the fulfillment—"something greater than Solomon is here" (Matt 12:42).

▸ Geopolitical Context

Relative weakness of Egypt and Mesopotamia allows Israelite expansion. David subdues Philistia, Moab, Edom, Ammon, Aram. Solomon engages in international diplomacy. This brief golden age typologically foreshadows the Messiah's universal reign. The land promise reaches its fullest Old Testament extent (1 Kgs 4:21).

Epoch VIII
The Divided Kingdom
1 Kings 12–2 Kings 25 · c. 931–586 BC
931 BC The Kingdom Divides — Rehoboam & Jeroboam I ▸

Rehoboam's folly provokes secession of the ten northern tribes under Jeroboam I. The kingdom splits into Israel (North; capital: Samaria) and Judah (South; capital: Jerusalem). Jeroboam institutes golden-calf worship at Dan and Bethel—rival sanctuaries. Every subsequent northern king is condemned: "he walked in the way of Jeroboam."

c. 874–853 BC Ahab, Jezebel & Elijah — Confrontation with Baal ▸

The Omride dynasty introduces Baal worship to the North. Elijah confronts the prophets of Baal on Mount Carmel (1 Kgs 18). Fire from heaven. Elijah flees to Horeb/Sinai, encountering God in the still small voice. Elijah is the prototypical covenant-enforcement prophet and forerunner-type (Mal 4:5; Matt 17:10–13). Elisha receives a double portion; his miracles anticipate Christ's.

c. 760–715 BC The Writing Prophets — Covenant Prosecutors ▸

The prophets function as covenant prosecutors, bringing YHWH's covenant lawsuit (rîb) against unfaithful Israel and Judah. Their pattern: charge of violation → announcement of curses → call to repentance → promise of restoration.

▸ Prophets to Israel (North)

Jonah (c. 780): Sign of three days; mission to Nineveh; God's mercy to nations. Amos (c. 760): Social justice; covenant lawsuit; Day of the Lord. Hosea (c. 755–715): Unfaithful bride; steadfast covenant love of God.

▸ Prophets to Judah (South)

Isaiah (c. 740–680): The Holy One of Israel; Immanuel (7:14); Mighty God child (9:6–7); Branch of Jesse (11); the four Suffering Servant songs (42, 49, 50, 52–53); new creation. The most extensive messianic prophecy in the Old Testament.

Micah (c. 740–700): Bethlehem prophecy (5:2); covenant lawsuit (6:1–8). Nahum (c. 660): Judgment on Nineveh/Assyria. Zephaniah (c. 640): Day of the Lord; remnant hope. Habakkuk (c. 608): "The righteous shall live by faith" (2:4).

Jeremiah (c. 626–586): Weeping prophet; New Covenant prophecy (31:31–34)—covenant written on hearts, full forgiveness, universal knowledge of God. The pinnacle of Old Testament covenantal promise.

722 BC Fall of Samaria — Assyrian Conquest of the North ▸

Assyria under Shalmaneser V / Sargon II conquers Samaria. The northern tribes are deported; the land is resettled with foreigners—the origin of the Samaritans. The covenant curses of Deuteronomy 28 are enacted. Kings of the last decades: Jeroboam II (793–753, prosperity amid corruption), Hoshea (732–722, last king).

c. 729–609 BC Judah's Reformers — Hezekiah & Josiah ▸

Hezekiah (729–686): Great reform; Assyrian siege of Jerusalem (701 BC, Sennacherib)—God miraculously delivers the city (2 Kgs 19:35). Manasseh (697–642): Worst apostasy—fills Jerusalem with blood. Josiah (640–609): Greatest reform; discovery of the Book of the Law (621 BC); Passover renewal. Yet too late: "The LORD did not turn from the burning of his great wrath" (2 Kgs 23:26). After Josiah, Judah falls swiftly.

▸ Key Kings of Judah

Rehoboam (931–913) · Asa (911–870, reform) · Jehoshaphat (872–848) · Athaliah (841–835, usurper) · Joash (835–796, temple repair) · Uzziah (792–740) · Ahaz (735–715, Assyrian vassal) · Hezekiah (729–686, great reform) · Manasseh (697–642, worst apostasy) · Josiah (640–609, greatest reform) · Jehoiachin (598–597, deported) · Zedekiah (597–586, last king)

▸ Nations & Empires

Assyria (dominant c. 900–612): God's rod of judgment against the North and a threat to Judah. Babylon (rising from c. 626): God's instrument of judgment against Judah, temple, and monarchy. Egypt: vacillating ally, ultimately unreliable. The prophets condemn reliance on Egypt over trust in YHWH (Isa 30–31).

The Exile
586 BC · The Babylonian Captivity · The Great Covenant Crisis

The fall of Jerusalem and destruction of Solomon's temple represents the most devastating crisis in Old Testament history—the fullest enactment of the Mosaic covenant curses (Lev 26:27–45; Deut 28:49–68). Every visible marker of God's covenant is stripped away:

What Was Lost

  • The Land — promised inheritance, forfeited
  • The Temple — dwelling of God, destroyed
  • The Throne — Davidic kingship, suspended
  • The City — Jerusalem, razed
  • The Cultus — sacrificial worship, halted

Seeds of Hope

  • Irrevocable Davidic covenant (Ps 89)
  • New Covenant promise (Jer 31:31–34)
  • The Suffering Servant (Isa 52:13–53:12)
  • Valley of dry bones (Ezek 37)
  • Promise of the Spirit (Ezek 36:26–27)
  • Restoration oracles (Isa 40–66)
▸ Exilic Prophets — Ezekiel & Daniel

Ezekiel (c. 593–571 BC): Among the exiles in Babylon. God's glory departs the temple (ch. 10) and returns to a new temple (ch. 43). Valley of dry bones (ch. 37): national resurrection, type of final resurrection. God Himself will shepherd His people (ch. 34)—fulfilled in Christ (John 10). New temple vision (chs. 40–48): eschatological dwelling of God.

Daniel (c. 605–530 BC): At Babylonian and Persian courts. Demonstrates YHWH's sovereignty over all kingdoms. Statue dream (ch. 2): successive empires culminating in God's indestructible kingdom. Son of Man vision (ch. 7:13–14): one "like a son of man" receives everlasting kingdom from the Ancient of Days—central to Christ's self-identification (Mark 14:62). Seventy weeks (ch. 9:24–27): timeline of redemption culminating in the Messiah.

▸ Exile as Type

The exile recapitulates Adam's expulsion from Eden. Sin → banishment from God's presence. Christ's crucifixion is the ultimate exile—He endures God-forsakenness (Matt 27:46) to end His people's exile. The return from exile typifies Christ's restoration: new exodus, new creation, return to God's presence. The exile is the crucible from which the hope of the gospel emerges.

Epoch IX
Post-Exilic Restoration
Ezra, Nehemiah, Haggai, Zechariah, Malachi · c. 539–430 BC
539 BC Cyrus's Decree — The Return Begins ▸

Cyrus of Persia conquers Babylon and permits the Jews to return and rebuild the temple—fulfilling Isaiah's prophecy naming Cyrus as YHWH's "anointed" shepherd (Isa 44:28–45:1). God uses a pagan king as His instrument. ~50,000 return under Zerubbabel (Davidic descendant). The altar is rebuilt; sacrifices resume.

516 BC Second Temple Completed ▸

After opposition and delay, prophets Haggai and Zechariah stir the people. The second temple is completed. But God's glory does not fill this temple as it did Solomon's—a conspicuous absence. Haggai promises: "The latter glory of this house shall be greater than the former" (2:9)—fulfilled in Christ's presence.

▸ Zechariah's Messianic Visions

The richest post-exilic Christology: the Branch who is both priest and king (3:8; 6:12–13); the king riding on a donkey (9:9, fulfilled Palm Sunday); the pierced one mourned (12:10); the shepherd struck (13:7); the fountain for sin (13:1). Joshua the High Priest clothed in filthy garments then clean robes (ch. 3)—a type of imputed righteousness.

458–445 BC Ezra & Nehemiah — Torah, Walls, & Reform ▸

Ezra (c. 458): Reestablishes Torah instruction; reforms intermarriage—covenant purification. Nehemiah (c. 445): Rebuilds Jerusalem's walls. Covenant-renewal ceremony (Neh 8–10). Ongoing reforms. The restoration is real but partial: the people return, the temple stands, the walls are rebuilt—but no Davidic king reigns, no glory descends, no Spirit is poured out. The Old Covenant structures function as reminders of promises still awaited.

c. 430 BC Malachi — The Last Prophetic Voice ▸

Confronts spiritual lethargy, corrupt priesthood, covenant unfaithfulness. Promises the coming of the Lord to His temple (3:1) and the messenger-forerunner—Elijah—before the Day of the Lord (4:5–6). After Malachi, prophetic revelation ceases for four centuries. The Old Testament ends in a sustained posture of waiting.

Epoch X
The Intertestamental Period
c. 430 BC – 5 BC · The Silent Centuries
331 BC Alexander the Great & Hellenization ▸

Alexander conquers the Persian Empire. Greek language, culture, and philosophy permeate the world. The Septuagint (LXX)—Greek translation of the Old Testament—is produced, providentially preparing for the gospel's spread in a common language. Palestine is caught between Ptolemies (Egypt) and Seleucids (Syria). The Jewish diaspora spreads synagogues throughout the Mediterranean world—the apostles' future mission field.

167 BC Antiochus IV & the Maccabean Revolt ▸

Antiochus IV Epiphanes desecrates the temple—altar to Zeus, swine sacrifice. The "abomination of desolation" (Dan 11:31), a type of the eschatological abomination (Matt 24:15). The Maccabean revolt achieves temporary independence. The Hasmonean dynasty illegitimately combines king and priest offices—deepening messianic longing for the true King-Priest.

63 BC Rome Conquers — The Fullness of Time Approaches ▸

Pompey conquers Jerusalem. Rome installs the Herodian dynasty. Herod the Great renovates the temple. The Pharisees, Sadducees, Essenes, and Zealots emerge. Messianic expectation intensifies. The Pax Romana provides roads, common language, legal system, and relative peace—the infrastructure for gospel proclamation. Every development is sovereignly orchestrated for the "fullness of time" (Gal 4:4).

Epoch XI — The Center of All History
The Incarnation & Ministry of Christ
Matthew–John · c. 5 BC – AD 30 · The Fullness of Time
c. 5 BC The Incarnation — The Word Becomes Flesh ▸

"The Word became flesh and dwelt among us" (John 1:14). The eternal Son of God assumes true human nature, born of the Virgin Mary in Bethlehem (Mic 5:2; Luke 2:4). The genealogies demonstrate descent from Abraham and David. In Christ, God "tabernacles" among His people—the antitype of Eden, tabernacle, and temple.

c. AD 27 Baptism & Temptation — Anointing of the Messiah ▸

Baptized by John in the Jordan. Heavens open, Spirit descends, Father declares: "This is my beloved Son" (Matt 3:17). Anointed as Messianic Prophet, Priest, and King. Then led into the wilderness for 40 days—recapitulating Israel's 40 years. Where Adam fell and Israel failed, Jesus succeeds. He overcomes Satan by the Word of God, demonstrating His fitness as the faithful covenant-keeper, the Last Adam.

c. AD 27–30 Public Ministry — The Kingdom Has Come ▸

"The kingdom of God is at hand; repent and believe in the gospel" (Mark 1:15). Jesus teaches with authority, performs signs of the kingdom (miracles), calls twelve apostles (reconstituting the twelve tribes), forgives sins, reveals the Father. The Sermon on the Mount: law of the kingdom. The parables: the nature of the kingdom in its "already/not yet" form.

▸ The Three Offices of Christ

Prophet: The greater Moses, who reveals the Father and speaks God's final Word (Heb 1:1–2; Deut 18:15). Priest: The Melchizedekian priest-king who offers Himself and ever lives to intercede (Heb 7–10). King: The Son of David enthroned at God's right hand, ruling the nations (Ps 2; Ps 110; Rev 19:15).

c. AD 30 The Cross — The Climax of Redemptive History ▸

Christ, the Lamb of God, bears the sins of the elect, satisfies divine justice, propitiates God's wrath, reconciles sinners to God (Rom 3:21–26; 2 Cor 5:21; 1 Pet 2:24). Every type, every shadow, every sacrifice, every promise converges on the cross. The temple veil is torn from top to bottom (Matt 27:51). Christ's cry—"My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"—is covenant exile in its ultimate, substitutionary form.

▸ Covenant Fulfillment

Christ fulfills the Covenant of Works by His active obedience—the perfect life imputed to believers. He inaugurates the New Covenant in His blood (Luke 22:20; Jer 31:31–34). Abrahamic: He is the singular Seed (Gal 3:16); in Him all nations blessed. Mosaic: He fulfills the law (Matt 5:17)—Passover, High Priest, sacrifice, temple. Davidic: eternal throne occupied (Luke 1:32–33; Acts 2:30–36).

▸ Fulfillment of All Types

Christ is the true Adam, the true Israel, the true Moses, the true Joshua, the true David, the true Solomon, the true Temple, the true Passover Lamb, the true Manna, the true Bronze Serpent, the true Day of Atonement, the true Ark, the true High Priest, the true Prophet, the true King, the true Bridegroom, the true Vine, the true Shepherd, the true Wisdom, the true Servant. Every Old Testament type finds its reality, its antitype, its telos in Him.

c. AD 30 Resurrection & Ascension — New Creation Begins ▸

The Resurrection: Raised bodily on the third day—vindicating His person, claims, and atoning work. The beginning of the new creation (2 Cor 5:17). "The firstfruits of those who have fallen asleep" (1 Cor 15:20).

The Ascension: Christ ascends to the Father's right hand. He reigns as exalted Lord over all things (Eph 1:20–22; Phil 2:9–11). He enters the heavenly Holy of Holies as great High Priest (Heb 9:24). Receives all authority in heaven and on earth (Matt 28:18). The ascension is the enthronement of the Davidic King in the heavenly Zion.

Epoch XII
The Apostolic Era
Acts–Revelation · c. AD 30–100 · The Latter Days
c. AD 30 Pentecost — The Spirit Poured Out ▸

The promised outpouring of the Holy Spirit (Joel 2:28–32; Acts 2). Tongues of fire and the gift of languages—the reversal of Babel. Peter declares: "This is what was uttered through the prophet Joel." The New Covenant is operative: the Spirit writes God's law on hearts, empowers witness, unites Jew and Gentile. Pentecost is the birthday of the New Covenant Church and inaugurates the "last days" (Acts 2:17; Heb 1:2).

▸ New Covenant Administration

Baptism replaces circumcision as the covenant sign (Col 2:11–12). The Lord's Supper replaces Passover as the covenant meal (1 Cor 11:23–26). The Church is organized with officers (elders and deacons), means of grace (Word, sacraments, prayer), and discipline. The New Covenant community is no longer ethnically restricted but includes believers from every nation (Eph 2:11–22; Rev 5:9–10).

c. AD 33–60 Apostolic Mission — To the Ends of the Earth ▸

From Jerusalem to Judea to Samaria to the ends of the earth (Acts 1:8). Stephen's martyrdom (Acts 7). Conversion of Paul—apostle to the Gentiles. Jerusalem Council (Acts 15)—Gentiles are full covenant members without circumcision. Paul's missionary journeys establish churches across Asia Minor, Macedonia, Achaia. The gospel reaches Rome. The Abrahamic promise to bless all nations is being fulfilled.

▸ Typological Fulfillment

New Exodus: Deliverance from sin and Satan (Col 1:13–14). New Israel: "The Israel of God" (Gal 6:16), Abraham's offspring by faith (Gal 3:29). New Temple: The Church, built on apostles and prophets with Christ as cornerstone (Eph 2:19–22; 1 Pet 2:4–5). New Creation: Believers are a "new creation" (2 Cor 5:17), firstfruits of cosmic renewal.

AD 70 Destruction of Jerusalem — End of the Old Order ▸

Rome under Titus destroys the temple. Prophesied by Jesus (Matt 24:1–2; Luke 21:20–24). The definitive end of the Old Covenant sacrificial system. The typological temple has given way to the reality: Christ and His Church are the true temple. No further sacrifice is needed or possible (Heb 10:18).

c. AD 95 Revelation — The Church Between the Ages ▸

John's apocalyptic vision on Patmos. Christ walks among the seven churches (Rev 2–3). The Lamb opens the scroll of history. The Church endures tribulation, faithful witness, and persecution under the Roman Empire—the beast. The book culminates in the final victory, the marriage supper of the Lamb, and the New Jerusalem. The canon of Scripture closes. The Church now lives between the advents, armed with the complete Word of God, indwelt by the Spirit, engaged in the Great Commission.

Epoch XIII — The Eschatological Horizon
The Consummation
Revelation 19–22 · The End and Goal of All Things
The Last Day The Second Coming of Christ ▸

Christ returns visibly, bodily, and gloriously (Acts 1:11; 1 Thess 4:16; Rev 1:7). The blessed hope of the Church (Titus 2:13). All the dead—righteous and unrighteous—are raised bodily (John 5:28–29). The righteous glorified, conformed to Christ's body (Phil 3:21; 1 Cor 15:42–49). Final judgment: every thought, word, and deed (Matt 25:31–46; Rev 20:11–15). Believers acquitted on the basis of Christ's imputed righteousness. The wicked condemned.

Eternity New Heavens & New Earth — The New Jerusalem ▸

Creation renewed and glorified—not annihilated but liberated (Rom 8:19–23; 2 Pet 3:13; Rev 21:1). The New Jerusalem descends—perfected bride of Christ, eternal dwelling of God with His people (Rev 21:2–4).

▸ Covenant Consummation

Every promise reaches ultimate realization. The eschatological life Adam failed to secure is bestowed by the Last Adam. The Abrahamic seed is fully gathered—a multitude from every nation (Rev 7:9). The land expands to encompass the renewed creation (Rom 4:13). The Davidic throne is eternal. The New Covenant is perfected: law on glorified hearts, God fully known, sins remembered no more. The divine presence—mediated through Eden, tabernacle, temple, incarnation, Spirit—is now immediate: "They will see his face" (Rev 22:4).

▸ Typological Consummation

Eden restored and surpassed: tree of life reappears (Rev 22:2) in a city—cultural mandate fulfilled, garden become garden-city. No temple, "for its temple is the Lord God the Almighty and the Lamb" (Rev 21:22). The Sabbath rest entered fully (Heb 4:9–11). The marriage of the Lamb (Rev 19:7–9) consummates the bridal typology from Eve through Israel through the Church.

▸ The Trajectory of Scripture

From creation to new creation. Garden to city. First Adam to Last Adam. Covenant of works to covenant of grace to covenant consummated. Promise to fulfillment. Type to antitype. Shadow to substance. At every point, the center is Christ—the Alpha and the Omega (Rev 22:13). He is the One in whom all the promises of God are Yes and Amen (2 Cor 1:20). All enemies subdued. Death destroyed (1 Cor 15:26). God is all in all (1 Cor 15:28).
